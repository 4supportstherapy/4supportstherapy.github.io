Learning That Heals. Teaching That Transforms.

Just like counselling, teaching is not just standing in front of a class and talking. It is more: it is about disseminating information with the clear aim of learning taking place.

That means someone can talk for hours or minutes, depending on class time — and unfortunately, teaching and learning can still fail to take place. For learning to truly happen, the teacher needs to use a deliberate set of skills and strategies.

Effective learning happens when Knowledge, Skills, and the right Attitudes (KSA) are acquired. For this to happen, the teacher must draw from proven Learning Frameworks:

This approach is grounded in a blended theoretical framework that integrates both classical learning theories and contemporary inclusivity models. This reflects the reality of today’s classrooms: inclusivity spans pedagogy, curriculum, and school-level policy.

The Contemporary Inclusive Models:

1. Universal Design for Learning (UDL) - Meyer et al., 2014
We proactively design instruction to accommodate learner variability by offering multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression. In practice, this means students can demonstrate competence through oral presentations, visual projects, or hands-on tasks — not just written tests.

2. Inclusive Pedagogy Model - Florian, 2015
We treat diversity as the norm and challenge teaching practices that marginalize learners. Teachers have a responsibility to design tasks where all students participate meaningfully — for example, adapting group activities so students of varying abilities all contribute.

3. Policy Implementation Theory - Lipsky, 2010
Teachers and school leaders are “street-level bureaucrats” who interpret national policies in practice. This explains why curriculum goals are not always realized consistently across schools — and why teacher training in policy translation matters.

Classical Supporting Theories That Inform Best Practice:

Vygotsky’s Sociocultural Theory, 1978 - Learning is shaped by social interaction and cultural tools. This aligns with collaborative classrooms. Home environment is key to learning too.

Piaget’s Constructivism, 1972 - Developmental readiness matters. Learner-centered approaches must match students’ cognitive stages of Development. This emphasizes Cognitive readiness for learning.

Bruner’s Scaffolding Theory, 1966 - Teachers provide structured support that gradually decreases as learners gain independence. A learner is a key participant of learning.

Kolb’s Experiential Learning Cycle, 1984 - Knowledge emerges through cycles of doing, reflecting, and applying — the heart of competency-based learning. Learners should be encouraged to practice learnt skills outside the classroom.

Gardner’s Multiple Intelligences, 1993 - Recognizing diverse learner strengths validates a holistic approach to education.

Humanistic Theories - Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs: learners must feel safe and valued before they learn. At the entry they need to establish the goal/(need)they seek to achieve.

3-Level Model: Inclusivity as an Ecosystem

inclusivity should not be isolated classroom tricks: it Should operate across three levels:

1. Pedagogical Level
Inclusivity is enacted through differentiated instruction and responsive classroom practices, guided by inclusive pedagogy and sociocultural insights.

2. Curricular Level
Inclusivity is advanced through adaptation and learner engagement strategies informed by UDL, experiential learning, and multiple intelligences.

3. Policy & Leadership Level
Inclusivity is shaped by how school governance and leadership interpret and apply national frameworks — analyzed through Lipsky’s lens of policy implementation.

This three-level model ensures inclusivity is a systemic alignment of pedagogy, curriculum, and school leadership. It reflects the ambition of CBC (Competency Based Curriculum) reform: reorienting schools into inclusive learning communities capable of delivering equitable outcomes for all learners.
